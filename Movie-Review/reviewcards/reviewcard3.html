Jail Movie Review : Jail is an underwhelming, in-your-face message movie

The story is narrated through three youngsters — Karna (GV Prakash Kumar), a small-time criminal, Rocky (Nandhan Ram), a drug pusher, and Kalai (Pasanga Pandi), who wants to lead a normal life after his release from juvenile prison. Their area, Kaveri Nagar, is a resettlement colony for the slum dwellers in the city, and has gained notoriety as a breeding ground of criminals as crime seems to be an easier lifestyle for some of these people compared to going all the way to the city for work. And it doesn't help that the police, led by corrupt cop Perumal (Ravi Maria), frequently foist fake cases on them. Rocky's tussle with a rival gang in the area leads to loss of life and also drags his two friends into a cesspool of violence

Then, there are portions that feel so calculated, as if the director is ticking boxes. We have mother sentiment, courtesy Karna's mother Paapammal (Radikaa Sarathkumar); sister sentiment, thanks to Rocky's sister Mariapushpam (Jennifer); and of course, the friend sentiment. We even get a scene where the three friends huddle together and talk about their plight, lest we didn't get it from the storytelling. It's as if Vasantabalan doesn't trust his audience to understand the situation his characters are in.

The romantic track between GV Prakash and Abarnathi, who plays Rosamalar, a spunky new girl who has taken refuge in the locality, feels very wannabe, trying hard to be raw and real, but not succeeding in that attempt because of its affected nature. In contrast, the stunts, choreographed by Anbariv, actually feel raw and real, giving us the feeling of witnessing a street brawl. Some moments, like the track involving Kalai, which shows how this situation keeps preventing even those who want to escape it, also work well.

But the climactic portions are terribly contrived. They actually give us the sense that the filmmaker realised that his narrative wasn't going anywhere as every 'message' that needed to be conveyed had been told in the first half itself, and rushes to the conclusion, giving us scenes that are too simplistic. Rather than sending chills, the climax only evokes a cold indifference
